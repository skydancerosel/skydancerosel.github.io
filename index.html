Research Overview

My research applies geometric and dynamical-systems methods, developed through earlier work on geometric flows, to the study of neural network training dynamics, capability emergence, and representation structure.

In my past life as a mathematician, I studied how nonlinear flows concentrate, bifurcate, and reorganize under curvature and constraint. I now use analogous tools to analyze how gradient-based learning trajectories evolve, stabilize, and undergo phase transitions in large-scale neural networks.

This perspective treats training as a structured dynamical system, rather than solely an optimization process, and focuses on the geometric mechanisms through which new computational capabilities emerge.

Research Focus

My work aims to develop a principled, quantitative understanding of learning dynamics in modern neural networks. In particular, I study:

  • Low-dimensional structure of optimization trajectories and execution manifolds
  • Transverse instabilities and saddle-mediated transitions in generalization
  • Phase structure induced by regularization, architecture, and data distribution
  • Superposition and distributed algorithmic representations
  • Mechanistic structure in compositional and multi-step reasoning tasks

Through controlled experiments and scalable analysis tools, I seek to identify early-warning signals, invariants, and causal mechanisms underlying capability formation.

A central objective is to understand how new behaviors arise during pretraining, fine-tuning, and continual learning, and how these processes can be monitored and guided.

Why This Matters

As neural networks scale, new capabilities often emerge abruptly and unpredictably. While scaling laws and benchmark evaluations describe performance trends, they provide limited insight into the internal dynamics that govern when and how these transitions occur.

By analyzing the geometry of training trajectories and their instabilities, my research aims to provide tools for:

  • Detecting emergent capabilities during training
  • Monitoring regime changes in long-running or adaptive systems
  • Improving robustness and controllability of learned behavior
  • Supporting interpretability and alignment efforts upstream of deployment

This approach complements activation-based interpretability and post-hoc analysis by focusing on the formation of internal structure during learning.
